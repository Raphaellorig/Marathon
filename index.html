🌍Culture & Marathon History

The legend of the marathon dates back to the Battle of Marathon in 490 BC,when the Greeks repelled the Persian invasion. According to Herodotus, a messenger named Pheidippides allegedly ran the ~40 km from Marathon to Athens to announce the victory, collapsing after shouting: "We have won!". While historians debate the exact truth, this myth became the foundation of modern distance running.

At the 1908 London Olympics, organizers wanted the race to start from Windsor Castle so the royal children could watch the start, and finish right under the balcony of Queen Alexandra in the Olympic stadium. The measured distance was 42,195 km. This distance was officially adopted as the worldwide standard by the IAAF in 1921 — and it has never changed since.

Every year, more than 800 marathons are organized worldwide, attracting approximately 1.1 million finishers. The six World Marathon Majors — Tokyo, Boston, London, Berlin, Chicago, and New York — are the most prestigious. The men's world record is held by Kelvin Kiptum (Kenya) at 2h00'35" in Chicago 2023. For women, Tigst Assefa (Ethiopia) set the mark at 2h11'53" in Berlin 2023.

  • Your feet strike the ground approximately 35,000 to 40,000 times during a marathon.
  • The ideal temperature for running a marathon is 8-12°C — below or above this, average times worsen significantly.
  • The pain felt at km 30 is as mental as it is physical — which is why mental preparation matters.
  • Bananas are the most distributed food at marathon aid stations worldwide.
  • Elite runners burn approximately 2,600-3,000 calories during a marathon.

Why Run a Marathon

Finishing a Marathon Means
Joining the 1%

Statistically, less than 1% of the world population has finished a marathon. This isn't just a number — it's concrete proof that you're capable of pushing past limits you never imagined possible.

A marathon isn't just a checkbox. It's a silent demonstration of discipline, perseverance, and courage — first to yourself, then to the world. Every km trained, every morning you chose to run despite fatigue, every energy gel at km 30 when your legs begged you to stop — all of it shaped something in you.

When you cross that finish line, be proud. You're part of a very select club.
